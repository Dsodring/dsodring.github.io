The Industry

By Daniel Sødring on November 6, 2019

Demand for developer and UX roles are growing despite Brexit, so it is still a good time to break into the industry, but as students we still need to put in a lot of work outside of class as shown in a Manchester Digital Skills Audit for 2019, the current curriculum is not up to date according to the industry.

In a survey done by Manchester Digital, 56% of businesses reported an increase of staffing levels and only 11% experienced a decline and the same businesses also reported that the roles with the most growth were developers, digital marketing and user experience. Web development and user experience are also the fields I am most interested and currently spend a lot of my spare time developing my skills in and plan on pursuing a career in after university.

What does it take to get a job?

Developer, AI, machine learning and user experience are the three fields most likely to grow. Although 74% of businesses recruit graduates, only 29% of them have an in-house scheme, making it more difficult to break into the industry successfully. One thing that will help, is having a well designed portfolio and the most common complaint was the recruitment process being too labour intensive and finding the right candidates was difficult (Manchester Digital, 2019). In a podcast with two Google employee’s (The Futur, 2019), Mustafa Kurtuldu, UX lead and Design Advocate at Google, outlines what a good UX portfolio should include and they discuss how recruiting for a UX role and having to go through their portfolio can be very time consuming, which supports the results from the Manchester Digital report for that field. That shows the importance of having a good and well designed portfolio to showcase your work and the process you have gone through for your products and projects.

60% of businesses responding to the survey by Manchester Digital, thought the current curriculum was not up to date. In the podcast by The Futur, they talk about design trends in 2019 and the Google employee’s mention they have been pushing mobile and a mobile-first approach for a long time now. But in order to develop mobile-first, you need to have a good understanding of CSS, more specifically CSS grid and flex, which we are learning.

I was talking to a friend who is a student at Manchester Metropolitan University, he told me they had coursework due and the requirements were to build a website with some simple content in it, we got talking about layouts using CSS and I mentioned grid and flex and he had no idea how to use those techniques. So there is definitely a gap somewhere, and as a student trying to break into the industry after graduation it is necessary to learn and do research outside your course to fully understand the industry trends and best practices.

What does Brexit mean for the tech industry?

A key point to touch on is Brexit and the uncertain circumstances around it. As an EU student it will certainly have an impact on my future and I am not sure if I will be staying in the UK to pursue a career or move back to Denmark or another country in Europe and try my luck there. In fact, Denmark is among the best to grow, attract and retain talent (Ministry of Foregin Affairs of Denmark, 2019) with Denmark taking the number three spot on the Global Talent Competitiveness Index (GTCI) in Europe, with the UK shortly after at 9th place. However when comparing cities, Copenhagen takes the top spot in the EU for its ability to compete for talents and is second in the world, only behind Washington DC.

The consequences of Brexit are noticeable, 37% of businesses reported a negative impact from the uncertainty surrounding Brexit and said it is more difficult to recruit from the EU (Manchester Digital, 2019). Similarly, according to a techUK report, between 65% and 77% of SMEs believe Brexit will have a fairly or very negative impact on their business, stating the main reason for this negative impact being lack of domestic talent due to high competition and difficulty recruiting talent from the EU or outside the EU due to the concerns surrounding a no deal Brexit.

SMEs also reported they were unable to properly prepare for Brexit due to the unpredictability of a no deal agreement. However despite the disappointing news, the UK tech industry is still growing fast and steadily, with a 61% growth in investment for UK scaleup tech firms between 2017 and 2018 (Tech Nation, 2019).
